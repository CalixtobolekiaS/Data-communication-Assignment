// 01 — Overview

What I Studied in
Data Communication

From what I've covered during Data Communication classes, the course starts by taking you back to where it all began. We looked at the telegraph - one of the earliest ways humans sent messages over long distances using electricity. The idea was simple: press a key, break a circuit, send a pulse. On the other end, someone listens to the clicks and decodes them using Morse code, a system of dots and dashes that represents letters and numbers. It sounds old-fashioned, but it laid the foundation for everything that came after.

From there, the course zooms out to look at the bigger picture of how data communication evolved. Before electricity, people used smoke signals, flags, and reflected light to pass information around. What's fascinating is that all of these systems, no matter how primitive followed the same basic structure: a sender, a medium, a receiver, and some form of encoding. That structure never went away. It just got faster, more reliable, and more complex over time, eventually giving us the digital networks we use today.

We then moved into how modern networks are actually built, starting with hub-based Ethernet. A hub is a shared medium, meaning all devices on the network compete to send data at the same time using a protocol called CSMA/CD to avoid collisions. When you introduce wireless into the mix, the rules change slightly — Wi-Fi uses CSMA/CA, which tries to avoid collisions before they happen rather than detecting them after. Connecting the two worlds is the job of an Access Point (AP), and a neat feature called PoE (Power over Ethernet) means the AP can receive both its data connection and its power through a single cable — no extra socket needed.

After networks, we looked up — literally — at satellite communication. A signal travels from a ground station up to a satellite, gets amplified and frequency-converted, then beams back down to a receiver somewhere else on Earth. Depending on how high the satellite orbits, it falls into one of three categories: GEO (fixed above one point, used for TV and weather), MEO (middle range, used for GPS), or LEO (low and fast, like Starlink). The coverage satellites offer is unmatched, but they come with real trade-offs — signal delay, weakening over distance, and very high costs.

The course wraps up with something that ties everything together: security. Specifically, we studied PGP (Pretty Good Privacy), a tool that encrypts messages and files so only the right person can read them. It combines asymmetric encryption (public and private keys) to share a secret key safely, and then symmetric encryption to actually scramble the data quickly. It also uses digital signatures to prove a message is genuine. The four security goals PGP addresses confidentiality, integrity, authentication, and non-repudiation are concepts that apply far beyond just PGP and are worth understanding deeply.

// 02 — Historical Diagram

The Electric Telegraph

The electric telegraph was the world's first practical electrical telecommunications system. Invented in the 1830s–40s, it sent coded pulses (Morse code) over a wire — the conceptual ancestor of every modern data network.

Key components: The Morse key (switch) at the sender interrupts the circuit to create short (dot) and long (dash) pulses. The electrical signal travels along insulated copper wire strung between wooden poles. At the receiver, an electromagnet sounder clicks, and a register prints the dots and dashes on paper tape.

// 03 — Key Concepts

Evolution of Data Communication

1837 - Electrical Telegraph

The First Network

Cooke & Wheatstone (UK) and Morse (USA) independently patent the electrical telegraph — the first system to transmit encoded information over distance using electricity.

1876 - Telephone

Analog Voice Transmission

Bell converts sound waves into continuous electrical signals, introducing analog communication and the concept of bandwidth as a constraint on signal fidelity.

1948 - Shannon's Information Theory

The Mathematical Foundation

Claude Shannon publishes A Mathematical Theory of Communication, defining channel capacity (C = B log₂(1 + S/N)), entropy, and the theoretical limits of data transmission.

1969 - ARPANET

Packet Switching & the Internet's Ancestor

The US DoD's ARPANET demonstrates packet switching — breaking data into labelled chunks routed independently — replacing circuit switching for data networks.

1983 - TCP/IP Standardised

The Internet Protocol Suite

TCP/IP becomes the universal standard, establishing the layered model (Application, Transport, Network, Link) that governs the internet to this day.

Today — 5G & Optical Fibre

Terabit-Speed Transmission

Modern networks combine dense wavelength-division multiplexing (DWDM) on fibre optics with 5G mmWave radio, achieving multi-terabit capacities globally.

Why Data
Communication
Matters to Me

"The single biggest problem in communication is the illusion that it has taken place."
— George Bernard Shaw

data communication is more than just a technical subject to me, it's a lens through which I see the world. The way information flows, the protocols that govern it, and the networks that carry it are all reflections of human ingenuity and our desire to connect. Understanding how data moves from one point to another gives me a deeper appreciation for the invisible infrastructure that powers our digital lives.

  • The idea that a signal can carry a message halfway around the world in milliseconds drives my curiosity about how networks are engineered.
  • Studying how Shannon defined the mathematical limits of communication showed me that even information has a physics.
  • Every protocol stack — from Morse to TCP/IP — is a creative solution to the same fundamental problem: reliable delivery.
  • I aspire to design systems where latency is near-zero and connectivity reaches communities that are currently offline.
